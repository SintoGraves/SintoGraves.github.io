## Discussion: What Congress Built
The H-1B visa was not written in isolation. It was part of a broad legislative design to balance business needs, worker protections, and national growth.  Congress debated how to let employers hire specialized talent without displacing domestic labor.  The result was a statute that defined both purpose and limit: a system that welcomed, but also watched.

## The Policy in Practice
Congress authorized reasonable filing fees to recover processing costs.  It did not authorize deterrent pricing.  A processing fee funds the system. A deterrent fee influences behavior.  One implements the law; the other reshapes it.  Supporters argue that a high fee limits misuse of the program and encourages domestic hiring.  Critics say it effectively rewrites congressional intent by turning access into privilege.

## The Broader Implication
When executive agencies set policies that alter economic outcomes, they tread into legislative ground.  The Constitution expects Congress to make the law and the Executive to enforce it.  Yet, as administrative power grows, oversight often trails behind.

The H-1B story reflects a larger question: how far can administration go before it becomes legislation in disguise?

---

## In Closing
The H-1B visa began as an idea of cooperation between two branches.  Today, it stands at the edge of interpretation.  The framework Congress built still exists, but the meaning within it is being rewritten, rule by rule, form by form. 
  
 *Do process changes strengthen the law, or slowly replace it?*
